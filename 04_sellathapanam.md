# *Selladha Panam* by Imayam

![Selladha Panam cover](https://m.media-amazon.com/images/S/compressed.photo.goodreads.com/books/1515780867i/37920093.jpg)

## How I Found The Book

The previous day, I was disappointed at the Tirunelveli book exhibition because I could not find any Tamil book with real literary value. It was full of stalls selling the same set of books. The only exception was the Lion Comics stall (which I know through Jeyamohan), where I bought a Tamil comic to try my hand.

Still hoping to find a good book, I went to Eagle Book Center. It was lunch time, and some staff members were sitting on the floor in a less-visited section, having their lunch. That corner happened to be the Tamil novel section. I was standing at nearby a shelf, overlooking and reading the titles. I found a few books by **Imayam**.

He is one of the Tamil authors who has received many recognitions (almost all. Only Gnanapeedam pending), though there are doubts that he gets extra attention mainly because of the dalit themes he writes about. I usually read novels set in South Tamil Nadu because I can fully relate to the context. He is from North Tamil Nadu. I had been wanting to read his work for some time. Since I saw his book there and knew that *Selladha Panam* had won the **Sahitya Akademi Award**, I bought it. I did not know what the novel was about when I purchased it.

## Short Summary

A studious girl from an upper middle class family, who has graduated from a premier engineering college (her father is a school headmaster and her brother is a team lead in a top IT company), falls in love with an uneducated, irresponsible, hot-tempered auto driver (his parents are refugees from Burma and have no stable income).

In spite of strong objections from her family, she marries him. Though she visits her mother occasionally and gets financial help, her father and brother have stopped talking to her.

One day, with **80% burn injuries**, she is admitted to the hospital. On the third day, she dies. What happens during those three days in the hospital is the focus of the novel.

## Reading It After Personal Trauma

If I had read this book even a month earlier, I would not have been able to relate to it and would probably have stopped after 20 pages, rejecting the events and the way the story progresses.

But I read it **immediately after going through a similar trauma** myself (ICU and death). I had already thought of writing down the things that happened, because I was not able to digest many of them and could not tolerate the insensitivity of some people. After reading this book, I felt as if someone had looked into my life and turned it into fiction.

That emotional overlap made the novel far more disturbing and believable to me.

## Part 1 – The Love Story

In Part 1 (the love story), I still feel there is **no strong reason** for her to fall in love with him. The attraction and the relationship are not convincing enough for me. (something missing in my life ?)

It feels as if the author mainly wants to **take the reader to the post–fire accident world**, and their love story is just a device to create that situation. The imbalance between their backgrounds is clear, but not enough to fall in love, neglecting family and marrying him.

## Part 2 – ICU, Death, and Human Behaviour

From Part 2 onwards, the novel portrays **how strange and harsh people can be** in crisis situations. Many readers might dismiss some scenes as exaggerated:

- Parents / family wishing their daughter be dead  
- Parents suddenly ready to do “anything” now, after having been indifferent before.  
- The ruthlessness and emotional coldness of an ICU ward (for visitors)

But I could agree with these depictions **101%**, because they echoed what I have personally seen. This part of the novel felt brutally real and at times, distrubing.

## Language and Dialect

The spoken dialect in the book did not fully convince me. Almost all the characters seem to speak in the **same voice**, regardless of their class, education, age, or profession. For a novel that depends so much on social differences, this lack of variation feels bit akward.

## Money That Becomes Worthless

Within 35–40 pages, shortly after Part 2 begins, **money becomes worthless** in the story. If the author had stopped there, it would already feel like a stretched short story.

However, he keeps **repeating the idea of money becoming worthless** again and again until the end. The theme is powerful, but the insistence on it feels redundant after a point.

When a short story is extended into a novel, I expect a certain **sense of completeness or fullness of life** (I am short of vocabulary). I missed that.

This is a must read book, which shows you another side of life and may be condition your mind for furture situations.
